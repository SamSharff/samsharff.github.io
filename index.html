Introduction

From theatre to public libraries to web development. My work has been varied, but not random. I've done A LOT of work with the public, troubleshooting tech questions, seeing what works, and--frequently--what doesn't. It's one of the reasons I got into tech in the first place, to be of use in the space where the public and technology intersect. Living in rural Maine, issues of digital literacy are especially near-and-dear to my heart, but I think you can use your powers for good in so many different ways, that I have benefitted from remaining open to what that work looks like.

I am an active member of Technologists for the Public Good, and co-creator of their first-ever book discussion group. Beyond that, I listen to a lot of audiobooks, watch a lot of baseball, and am mother to a fantastic kiddo who gives me hope for the world to come.

Currently, I'm working on...a resume database app for job seekers using Ruby/Vue/JS/PostgreSQL, learning ArcGIS software, and diving deeper into SQL in any spare time.

Experience

Teaching Assistant: Web Development

I'm a teaching assistant at Actualize Coding Bootcamp in their full stack web development program. This means I work a lot in Ruby, Rails, Vue.JS, JavaScript, PostgreSQL and more. I get to hang with the most wonderful people, guiding students as they learn new technologies/frameworks/tools, and hold virtual office hours to answer questions and keep rising panic at bay.

Libraries, Social Services, Theatre

I have several years under my belt at public libraries, doing research, helping patrons find materials, taking graduate-level courses, and of course troubleshooting countless technical issues with users. I also have worked with the homeless and those at-risk, and have consistently steered my professional life to work in some capacity on behalf of those who are struggling.

Parenting

Oh, I'm also a mom, so that's a trippy 24/7 learning experience--I'm used to being schooled by my silly, occasionally tyrannical six-year-old on exactly what I've done wrong, and how I should do it next time. In that context, I would say I receive constructive criticism pretty well and reeeeeally understand the urgency of keeping your sense-of-humor.
